## Term

A phrase you can use to position your product — the kind of language you might use in a landing page headline, tagline, meta title, or product description. A term describes a category or market people actually search for, like "waterproof hiking shoes" or "invoice software for freelancers." RankEarly groups close variations under the same term, so "best AI content writer" and "top AI content writer" both roll up to **AI content writer**.

Not a term: "shoes" (too broad), "how to choose waterproof hiking shoes" (that's a keyword cluster), "best waterproof hiking shoes for wide feet" (that's a variation).

## Keyword

A word or phrase that people type into a search engine. "Email deliverability" is a keyword. So is "how to improve email open rates." RankEarly tracks keywords so you can see what people are searching for and how hard it is to rank for each one.

## Keyword Cluster

A group of keywords that Google treats as the same query — meaning they return very similar search results. For example, "best project management tools," "top project management software," and "project management tools for teams" would likely belong to one cluster because Google shows nearly the same pages for all of them.

RankEarly uses SERP-similarity clustering: it compares the actual search results for each keyword, and if two keywords share most of the same top-ranking pages, they go into the same cluster. This way you don't waste time writing separate posts for queries that Google already answers with the same results.

## Topic

A broad subject area made up of related keyword clusters. Also called a **topic cluster**. For example, "project management" as a topic might include keyword clusters around task tracking, team collaboration, Gantt charts, and agile workflows.

A topic is built bottom-up from keyword clusters — it represents how your content maps to a subject area. We recommend writing one [pillar page](#pillar-page) per topic and following the [pillar-cluster model](#pillar-cluster-model), but it's not required. If a keyword covers a broad enough territory on its own, you can write a pillar page for that single keyword instead.

Since keyword expansion can't guarantee 100% coverage of every search demand, some keywords may not have direct long-tail queries derived from them. That's fine — you can still discover angles and sub-intents within a keyword and create a piece of content for each.

## Keyword Library

A collection of keywords and search results for a project. Most RankEarly workflows use it as input. See [Keyword Libraries](/keyword-library) for details.

## Winnability

A label RankEarly assigns to tell you whether an opportunity is worth targeting. You will see three labels: **Winnable** (realistic opening), **Challenging** (mixed signals), and **Skip** (no good opening right now). See [Winnability](/how-it-works/winnability) for how labels are applied in each workflow.

## Project

Where all your research lives. A project stores your business context — product description, target audience, competitors, business goals, and seed phrases. Inside a project you will find research history, saved items, and keyword libraries.

## Pillar-Cluster Model

A content strategy where one broad pillar page links to multiple focused cluster pages, and each cluster page links back to the pillar. The pillar covers the topic at a high level; the cluster pages go deep on each subtopic.

The recommended approach is to write cluster pages first — each targeting a specific keyword cluster — and then build the pillar page iteratively as your cluster content grows. This way the pillar has real depth to link to from day one. Also known as the [hub-and-spoke model](#hub-and-spoke-model).

## Hub-and-Spoke Model

Another name for the [pillar-cluster model](#pillar-cluster-model). The "hub" is the pillar page (covering the broad topic), and the "spokes" are the cluster pages (each covering a subtopic in depth). Every spoke links back to the hub, and the hub links out to all spokes — creating a tightly interlinked content structure that signals topical authority to search engines.

## Pillar Page

A page that covers a broad topic at a high level and links out to more detailed cluster pages on subtopics. It acts as the hub in a [pillar-cluster model](#pillar-cluster-model). For example, a pillar page on "Project Management" would summarize task tracking, team collaboration, and agile workflows — then link to dedicated blog posts that go deep on each.

## Information Gain

The unique value your content adds beyond what already ranks on page one. If every top result explains what SPF records are but none explain how to debug a failing SPF check, that debugging angle is your information gain.

RankEarly's `/content-researcher` skill finds these gaps by reading the top-ranking pages and surfacing questions they don't answer well.

## SERP

Search Engine Results Page — the page Google shows after you search for something. When we say "analyze the SERP," we mean looking at what's currently ranking for a given keyword: which pages appear, what they cover, and where the gaps are.

## Topical Authority

The idea that search engines trust sites that cover a subject thoroughly. A site with 15 well-linked posts on project management signals more expertise than a site with one general post. Building topical authority is the main reason to create [topics](#topic) rather than standalone posts.

## Under-Discussed Questions

Questions that searchers likely have but that the top-ranking pages don't answer well (or at all). These are surfaced by RankEarly's `/content-researcher` skill and represent opportunities — topics where your content can provide something new that existing results don't.
