SERP gap analysis is the engine behind RankEarly's winnability verdicts. Instead of relying on a single keyword difficulty score, it analyzes the actual top 10 search results for a keyword and evaluates six competitive signals to determine if there's a real opportunity.

Every keyword evaluated in [Primary Term](/research/primary-term), [Content Angles](/research/content-angles), and [Topic Clusters](/research/topic-clusters) goes through this process.

## The process

For each keyword:

1. **Fetch the top 10 Google results** — title, URL, and description for each
2. **Evaluate 6 signals** using AI — each signal is marked as an opportunity or a barrier
3. **Generate a verdict** — Winnable or Skip, based on signal counts

## The 6 signals

### 1. Small sites or forums in the top 10

**Are smaller, independent sites ranking?**

If small sites or forums appear in the top 10, it means Google doesn't require high domain authority to rank for this keyword. This is a strong indicator that a new site can compete.

- **Yes** = opportunity — the authority barrier is low
- **No** = harder — big brands dominate the results

### 2. Mixed search intent

**Are the top results answering different questions?**

When some results are "what is..." guides, others are "best tools" lists, and others are templates, it means Google isn't confident about the intent. You can target a specific angle and wedge in.

- **Yes** = opportunity — Google is unsure, leaving room for focused content
- **No** = harder — there's a clear standard you need to beat

### 3. Content format gap

**Is there a mismatch between what users want and what results provide?**

If a query implies users want a template, tool, or checklist, but the results are all generic blog posts, there's a format gap you can fill.

- **Yes** = opportunity — give users the format they're looking for
- **No** = neutral — results already match the expected format

### 4. Low-quality results

**Are the top pages thin or repetitive?**

When the top results feel like everyone rewrote the same article — same structure, same points, no original insight — there's a quality gap. Better content can outrank them.

- **Yes** = opportunity — more depth or originality wins
- **No** = neutral — existing content is solid

### 5. Strong competitors

**Is the SERP dominated by authoritative sources?**

Official documentation, .gov/.edu sites, and category pages from major platforms are hard to displace regardless of content quality.

- **Yes** = barrier — consider a different angle
- **No** = neutral — no insurmountable authority barriers

### 6. Click-stealing SERP features

**Will organic results actually get clicks?**

Even if you rank on page 1, ads, featured snippets, knowledge panels, and other SERP features can push organic results below the fold.

- **Yes** = barrier — low click-through rate even if you rank well
- **No** = neutral — organic results get fair visibility

## The verdict rule

A keyword is **Winnable** when:
- 3 or more signals show opportunity (positive)
- At most 1 signal shows a barrier (negative)

Otherwise, the keyword is **Skip**.

| Signal | Opportunity | Barrier |
|---|---|---|
| Small sites / forums ranking | Yes | — |
| Mixed search intent | Yes | — |
| Content format gap | Yes | — |
| Low-quality results | Yes | — |
| Strong competitors | — | Yes |
| Click-stealing SERP features | — | Yes |

<Note>
The first four signals can only be positive (opportunity) or neutral. The last two can only be negative (barrier) or neutral. A keyword needs enough opportunity signals without too many barriers.
</Note>

Each research mode aggregates keyword-level verdicts differently. See [winnability](/how-it-works/winnability) for how per-keyword results roll up into term, topic, and pillar-level verdicts.

|---|---|
