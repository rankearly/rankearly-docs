Primary Term research helps you find **positioning terms**: short market or category phrases you can use in headlines, taglines, landing pages, and product messaging. You start from an existing keyword library, choose either your saved project context or one focused content idea, and RankEarly returns a ranked list of analyzed terms.

## When to use it

- You're deciding how to position a product, feature, or site
- You want several viable category terms backed by search data
- You want to compare options before saving the ones that fit your messaging

## What counts as a term

Good terms are short market phrases:

- `waterproof hiking shoes`
- `invoice software for freelancers`
- `SOC 2 compliance software`

These are not good terms:

- A content title like `How to choose waterproof hiking shoes`
- A term that is too broad, like `shoes`
- A brand-led phrase like `Nike waterproof shoes`

Modifier variations such as `best AI content writer` and `top AI content writers` are grouped under the same core term instead of being treated as separate markets.

## Before you start

Open Primary Term research from your project home or the **Terms** page.

Before you run it:

- Select a keyword library that belongs to the same project
- Make sure the library already contains keywords

## How it works

<Steps>
  <Step title="Choose a library and source">
    Select the keyword library, then choose one source:

    - **Project Context** uses your saved project information
    - **Content Idea** uses one focused subject you enter

    Use **Project Context** when you want broader positioning ideas. Use **Content Idea** when you want terms around one narrow idea.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Extract candidate terms from library keywords">
    RankEarly uses the keywords in your selected library to identify candidate terms and the support queries related to each term.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Analyze stored SERP snapshots">
    Primary Term does not fetch live Google results during this workflow. It evaluates the SERP snapshots already stored in the selected library for the term and its support queries, using the same gap signals described in [winnability](/concepts#winnability).
  </Step>
  <Step title="Rank the terms">
    Each term gets a verdict from the whole cluster, not only the head term. RankEarly looks at the term and its support queries together, then ranks the analyzed terms so the strongest options appear first.

    | Verdict | Condition | Meaning |
    |---|---|---|
    | **Winnable** | 3+ winnable queries across the term cluster | Strong opportunity across multiple related queries |
    | **Challenging** | 1-2 winnable queries | Some support, but mixed competition |
    | **Hard to Win** | 0 winnable queries | No clear opening right now |
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Reading the results

The results page shows your terms in ranked order. Click any term to open the detail drawer, where you can review:

- A short **Why This Term** summary
- **Support Queries** that reinforce the term
- The term's own analysis
- Save or remove action

To go deeper, create a [Content Brief](/planning/content-brief) from an expanded support-query card inside the drawer. You do not start a brief directly from the term card itself.

## Saved terms and research history

Saved terms are project-wide, so you can come back to the strongest positioning options later. Research History on the **Terms** page lets you reopen past Primary Term runs.

## What to do with the results

Save the terms that fit your positioning best. The first result is the highest-ranked option, but the workflow is designed for you to keep multiple viable terms and compare them across pages, messaging, and campaigns.

Common next steps:

- Use saved terms in landing page headlines, taglines, feature page headers, and meta titles
- Turn strong support queries into content briefs so supporting content can reinforce the term
- Re-run the workflow with a different content idea or updated project context when your positioning changes

## Tips

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Keep your keyword library populated">
    Primary Term depends on the keywords and saved SERP snapshots already in the selected library. If the library is thin, your term set will be thin too.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Save more than one term">
    The first result is the best-ranked option, not an automatic final answer. Save a few strong terms and test them in messaging before committing.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Terms and topics do different jobs">
    A term is the market you want to own. A topic is a content piece you publish. Use [Topic Clusters](/research/topic-clusters) or [Content Angles](/research/content-angles) when you want article ideas that support your chosen term.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
