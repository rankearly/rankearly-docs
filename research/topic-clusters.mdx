title: "Topic clusters"
description: "Turn an existing keyword library into content pillars and subtopics you can prioritize for your content plan."
---

Topic Clusters turns an existing keyword library into a structured content plan. It helps you analyze the keyword landscape around your project, group related search intents into article-sized subtopics, and prioritize the pillars that best fit what you want to rank for.

Each subtopic is one article opportunity. Each pillar is the broader theme those articles support.

## When to use it

- You want a full content plan, not a single article idea
- Your project already has a keyword library and you want to turn it into pillars
- You want to understand which themes are most worth building content around

## Before you start

Before you run it:

- Choose a keyword library that belongs to the same project
- Make sure the library already contains keywords and saved SERP snapshots
- Use [Keyword Expansion](/research/keyword-expansion) first if the project does not have a usable library yet

## How it works

<Steps>
  <Step title="Start from an existing keyword library">
    Topic Clusters uses the keywords already stored in your keyword library instead of starting from scratch.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Cluster related keywords">
    RankEarly compares the stored SERP snapshots in that library and groups related keywords into clusters. Each cluster becomes one subtopic, which usually maps to one article to write.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Organize clusters into pillars">
    AI reviews the keyword clusters and groups the related subtopics into content pillars.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Prioritize what to write">
    Review the returned pillars and their subtopics to decide which themes best fit your project, your audience, and the areas where you can publish credible experience-backed content.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Reading the results

Review each pillar as a theme and each subtopic as an article opportunity within that theme.

Use the subtopic metrics and underlying keyword clusters to decide:

- which pillars best align with your project
- which pillars best align with your project and expertise
- which subtopics are the best first articles to write
- which opportunities should move into a [content brief](/planning/content-brief)

## Saved pillars and research history

You can save the pillars you want to keep working from and revisit past Topic Clusters runs through research history later.

## What to do with the results

- Save the pillars that match your product, audience, and expertise
- Start with subtopics that combine lower KD with stronger search volume
- Create a [content brief](/planning/content-brief) from the subtopics you plan to write next
- Run Topic Clusters separately for different features or verticals if one library covers multiple themes

## Tips

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Use the KD filter for cleaner early-stage plans">
    The filter is on by default. It removes keywords above `KD 70`, which usually produces a more realistic plan for newer sites. Turn it off when you want the full market view or your site can compete on harder terms.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Topic Clusters depends on your library quality">
    Topic Clusters reuses the keywords and SERP snapshots already saved in your library. If the library is thin or outdated, the pillars will be thin too.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Run it per feature or vertical">
    If one project covers several features, industries, or audiences, run Topic Clusters on a focused library for each one. That keeps the pillars easier to name, save, and brief.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Topic Clusters vs Content Angles">
    Use **Topic Clusters** when you want to plan what to write across a whole theme. Use [Content Angles](/research/content-angles) when you already know the topic and want the best framing for one article.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
